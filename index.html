Case study

The rise and fall of YSRCP

Formed in 2011, YSRCP came from nowhere to run TDP close in 2014, swept to a historic landslide in 2019, then suffered one of the steepest reversals in Indian state election history in 2024 — despite barely losing any of its underlying vote.

A secondary factor

Where a residual Congress vote cost YSRCP the seat

YSRCP finished runner-up in 164 of 175 seats it didn't win in 2024. In most of those, the winning margin was too large for any spoiler to matter. But in 9 of them, the third-place candidate alone won more votes than YSRCP's losing margin — meaning if that candidate's voters had gone to YSRCP instead, the seat would have flipped. 8 of the 9 spoilers were Congress (INC), not a minor party at random: Rayalaseema — where most of these seats sit, including Kadapa, YS Jagan's own home turf — was Congress's stronghold before the 2011 YSRCP split, and it still holds a residual vote there that never fully consolidated behind YSRCP.

Scale check, so this isn't overstated: even in the best case, recovering all 9 of these only takes YSRCP from 11 seats to 20 — nowhere close to the 151 it held in 2019. Widen the test to every non-winner vote combined (not just 3rd place), and it's 16 seats, not 9. This confirms the earlier finding rather than replacing it: the 2024 collapse was overwhelmingly a broad swing to a consolidated NDA alliance, with Congress vote-splitting a real but minor secondary factor in a handful of close Rayalaseema races.
